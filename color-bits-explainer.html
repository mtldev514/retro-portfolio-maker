Understanding Color Channels

In the beginning, computers didn't use RGB (Red, Green, Blue) at all! They started with just 1 channel (black & white), then gradually evolved to use 3 separate channels (RGB) to create millions of colors.

Each era had different constraints: memory was expensive, processors were slow, and displays had physical limitations. Let's journey through time! üëá

1950s - 1970s

1 Channel: Monochrome Era

Early computers used 1 single channel - just intensity, no color! Think of it like a dimmer switch: you could only control how bright or dark a pixel was.

1-bit monochrome: 1 bit per pixel = 2 values (black or white)
Examples: Early terminals, original Macintosh (1984)

1987 - 1990s

8-bit: The Palette Era

A clever trick! Instead of RGB channels, use 8 bits to pick from a palette of 256 pre-selected colors. Games like Super Mario and Sonic used this!

8-bit indexed color: 8 bits per pixel = 256 colors from a palette
Common palette: 3-3-2 RGB (3 bits red, 3 bits green, 2 bits blue)
Examples: VGA, GIF files, SNES, Sega Genesis
8-bit palette (256 colors)

üí° Why palettes? Storing RGB values for every pixel took too much memory. Palettes let you pick 256 "favorite" colors and reference them with just 1 byte!

1990s

24-bit RGB: "True Color"

The modern standard! 8 bits per channel, evenly distributed. This gives us more colors than the human eye can distinguish!
